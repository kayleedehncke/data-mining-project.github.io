Conclusion

Non-Technical Summary

This project examined why heat-related events occur in the United States and whether they can be predicted using a combination of weather data, seasonal patterns, and community vulnerability indicators. By merging daily temperature records from NOAA, disaster declarations from FEMA, and vulnerability metrics from the CDC Heat & Health Index, the project created a unified dataset covering nearly 200,000 station-day observations across 40 states from 2020–2024.

Four different analytical approaches were used to understand heat risk from multiple angles. Logistic regression and decision trees attempted to predict when a heat event would occur, while K-means clustering explored whether natural groups of conditions emerged in the data. FP-growth association rule mining identified simple, interpretable patterns that frequently co-occur with heat events during warm months.

Across all methods, the same conclusion emerged: heat events are not driven by temperature alone. High temperatures matter, but so do factors such as poverty, health burdens, low tree cover, and other indicators of community vulnerability. The models also confirmed strong seasonal patterns, with most heat events occurring between June and September.

Overall, the analysis shows that heat-related emergencies arise from a combination of environmental exposure and social vulnerability. Understanding these interactions can help communities better prepare for extreme heat and reduce the risks associated with it.

Key Insights and Findings

Several high-level insights emerged consistently across the notebooks.

Rare events are rare but not patternless. The positive class is less than 1% of the full dataset, which makes the task difficult, but the models do not behave randomly. ROC-AUC values near 0.79 for logistic regression and 0.89 for the final decision tree show that the feature set contains real predictive information.

Seasonality is a major driver. Monthly event rates rise sharply in June and remain highest through September. The decision tree ranked dayofyear, day, year, and weekday among the most important features, and the FP-growth notebook focused on May through September because that period contained nearly three quarters of the positive events.

Vulnerability matters alongside temperature. Weather alone does not explain the results. overall_score, hhb_score, poverty rate, asthma prevalence, imperviousness, and tree cover repeatedly appeared as informative variables. This supports the substantive interpretation that heat-event risk reflects the interaction between exposure and community context.

Leakage prevention is essential. The removed disaster-count variables were too close to the target to be used honestly in prediction. Their exclusion makes the problem harder, but also makes the final results more credible.

Real-World Impacts

The findings from this project have several meaningful real-world implications. First, the models highlight combinations of temperature, season, and vulnerability that precede heat events, which can support more targeted early-warning systems. Agencies could issue alerts tailored to communities with higher vulnerability scores rather than relying on broad statewide warnings.

Second, the results reinforce the importance of equitable climate adaptation. Communities with higher poverty, worse health outcomes, or limited tree cover face greater heat risk, suggesting that cooling centers, shade infrastructure, and green-space investments should be prioritized in these areas.

Third, emergency planners can use cluster-based insights to anticipate which regions are likely to experience heat stress even before events occur. This can improve resource allocation, staffing, and public-health preparedness.

Finally, the association-rule findings show that environmental factors such as impervious surfaces and low tree cover frequently co-occur with heat events. This supports urban-planning strategies like reflective roofing, increased vegetation, and heat-resilient infrastructure to reduce long-term risk.

Limitations and Threats to Validity

This project has several limitations. The first is the extreme rarity of the positive class. Even the stronger supervised results come with low precision, which means many flagged observations are false positives. That is not a bug in the evaluation; it is a direct consequence of the base rate. In real deployment, threshold choice would need to depend on the cost of misses versus false alarms.

Second, the supervised models used stratified random splits rather than a strictly forward-looking temporal split. This was faithful to the notebook implementations, but it likely makes the evaluation somewhat more optimistic than a true forecasting setup in which future years are held out entirely. A stronger future design would use rolling-origin evaluation or year-based backtesting.

Third, some highly weighted features are geographic indicators such as station or state dummies. These can be useful within-sample, but they may also absorb location-specific regularities that do not generalize cleanly to unseen stations or policy contexts. Similarly, the cluster profiles and FP-growth rules are descriptive summaries of this particular merged dataset rather than universal statements about heat risk.

Fourth, the FP-growth results depend heavily on discretization. Changing the binning rule or the selected features could change the discovered itemsets. K-means has a related limitation: its Euclidean-distance objective assumes roughly spherical clusters in standardized feature space, which may not perfectly reflect the true geometry of the data.

Finally, the project studies heat-related events at the granularity of station-day observations joined with state-level vulnerability features. That means some important local context is smoothed out. More granular population, land cover, humidity, or lagged tempera ture information could improve both prediction and interpretation.

Improvements and Future Work

There are several natural directions for extending this project. A first improvement would be to move from random stratified splitting to a temporal validation scheme in which entire future periods are held out. That would better match the real use case of anticipating future heat-related events. A second improvement would be to incorporate additional weather features such as multi-day heat accumulation, humidity, nighttime temperature, or persistence metrics. Heat impacts often depend on sustained exposure rather than one-day TMAX alone.

A third extension would be to calibrate the supervised models and explicitly optimize decision thresholds for different policy goals. For example, an emergency-preparedness setting might accept lower precision in exchange for higher recall, while a limited-resource intervention setting might require high precision. A fourth extension would be to compare the current interpretable models with more complex methods such as random forests or gradient boosting. Those methods were not part of this project, but they could serve as future baselines once the current leakage-aware workflow is established.

Finally, the unsupervised side of the project could be enriched by cluster stability analysis, alternative clustering algorithms, and multi-item antecedents in association-rule mining. These additions would not replace the current notebooks, but they would make the descriptive analysis more robust.
